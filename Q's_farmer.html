Q's Farmer App

UI/UX

Overview

Q's Farmer App is an app more than a traditional digital marketplace. It provides a platform for farmers and customers to trade and communication. Moreover, it aims to maintain social ties between farmers and customers in both the digital and the real world. The project includes a complete process of UX research, solution ideation and UI design. I did the research part as a group and the design part by myself.

Process

Background

A farmers' market is a physical retail marketplace intended to sell foods directly by farmers to customers. There are several benefits to farmers’ markets. For example, by directly selling products to the customers, farmers gain more profits than selling it to wholesalers, food processors, or large grocery firms. Customers also receive fresher and healthier foods meanwhile reduce the cost and pollution.

Farmers hold different kinds of events for people to get closer to nature and deliver knowledge of our daily food to everyone. For instance, A small farm in Austin cooperates with schools to teach kids where our food comes from and teach them how to grow their own plants. In other words, farms play the role of educators and provide a space for leisure.

Challenge

For small and medium-scale farmers, they have trouble maintaining their inventory due to several reasons. They also suffer from unnecessary losses because of the characteristics of fresh vegetable and fruit. Thus, farmers seek a new way to sell their items to customers, meet customer’s needs and also maintain the social connection at the same time.

User Research

Contextual Inquiry & Observations

We conducted contextual inquiries on farmers and customers in the farmers' market. By observing their interaction directly and recording their attitude, mood, reaction during real tasks, it brought us the information we couldn’t retrieve from the interviews. Through notes, photos and videos from different interviewers and perspectives, we were able to discuss and integrate the opinions afterward and finally catch the core values of that users care.

We used an affinity diagram to list our notes.

Interview

We conducted 7 semi-structured interviews as well, including the manager of 1 medium-scale farm, 2 farmers and 4 customers. We regarded the manager of the medium-scale farm as a key informant who can provide us the most insights about farmers. Moreover, we interviewed 4 different types of customers as well. Different from contextual inquiry, semi-structured interviews provided us information in a narrow scale. With the information in a restricted scale, we spotted the common difficulties or problems that customers face in traditional farmers' markets.

Challenges of farmers

We identified three challenges of farmers form the interview with the farm manager and farmers.

  • Unpredictable harvest
    Due to weather or other factors, harvest of the farmer is unpredictable.

  • Short shelf life
    The products are mainly fresh food which have a short shelf life.

  • Inventory update
    Inventory data needs to be update frequently because of unpredictable harvest and short shelf life.

Core values that customer cares

Deprived from contextual inquiries and interviews, we defined three core values that the customer cares the most.

"Flexibility"

Customers desire flexibility about the schedule of farmers' markets. There is usually a fixed schedule of farmers' markets. For example, the farmers' markets in Austin are held on Sunday from 10:00 am to 2 pm at Browning Hangar Park. However, not everyone is always available at that time. Thus, the customer seeks some flexibility to buy items at different times and locations.

"Convenience"

A customer seeks convenience when it comes to ordering products from farmers. Traditionally, customers can either make a phone call or go to the farm directly to order products. Nonetheless, there are only few or even only one worker on the farm during the weekday. Most of the time, they are all working on the farm instead of staying in the office and waiting for a phone call. Thus, making an order is a pain point for customers.

"Predictability"

Customers care about what kinds of products will they have in this week's market. Customers look for information for them to do other decisions since they still purchase fresh goods from other marketplaces. If they can know what will farmers have in this week's farmers market earlier, they will have a clear plan of what should buy or not buy during other shopping.

Brainstorming

At the first of ideation, we started from discussing with the farmers. We all agreed that mobile app is the option with the most potential. Next, we did brainstorm to come up with what functions shall we include in our app.
Based on our research, we came out of 50+ function first and chose those fitted the most in our user flow. Over iterate over and over, we came out of five functions.

Five Main Functions

Farmers can add items for customers to buy in this section. It works based on calendar. For example, farmers can add a item for 10 days and customer can view and order it only in these days.

A section not only for users to reserve events and farmers to creat events but also serves as a platform to promo information between each side

A farming simulator motivates users check the app frequently. When users harvest something on the app, they can gain same product in real world from farmers. We add weather and other condition in the game to create empathy and connection to farmers

Users have to cook almost everything that buy from the farm. Therefore, recipe serves as an useful tool to attract users to come back after purchasing

User and can send message directly to farmers, vice versa. It helps both sides to communicate efficiently and easily

For users, it's a traditional shopping cart where they can temporarily keep their product and eventually check out. For farmers, they can check, accept and reject orders

Three Goals

We listed three goals as well:

• Design an intuitive and user-friendly experience that users can efficiently place their order or reserve for events

• Provide a platform which allows farmers to update and sell their inventory easily and efficiently

• Create and maintain connection between customer and farmer

User Testing

Conducting user testing is always the most direct way to validate my design and I conducted two rounds of user testing.

The first round was testing the task flow and information architecture with wireframes. In order to discover potential breakdown and deadlock before hi-fi prototypes, I conducted paper prototype testing with lo-fi wireframes, it helps users to focus on fundamental flow, information architecture and users' needs without distracting them by images, colors, etc.

The second round was testing the whole app with hi-fi clickable prototypes on Figma. By observing users perform each task while thinking out loud, I received direct feedback and thoughts about what's working for them, what isn’t. Users' opinions are very valuable to me.  In this way, I found specific ways to improve users' experience.

Reflection

“Connection between people” is always the first priority in the development of this app.

One of the biggest benefits of local farmers' markets is to create social ties and link people together. Farmers' markets help create distinction and uniqueness, which can increase pride and encourage residents to engage in community. However, this kind of connection may be easily destroyed by the Internet. Thus, how to maintain this connection is my first priority. In the future, the function for customers to choose products with different levels of ripeness will be helpful. I hope farmers' markets could grow into a hub of a community where people can find healthy foods and a wealthy mood.
